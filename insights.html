Personal Narrative Final Drafts

Lessons learned from failure, pressure, and systematic thinking

The Engineer's Mindset

"Engineering isn't just putting metals together. It's structure, the way you think, how you analyze and troubleshoot under duress."

This realization came to me not in a classroom or laboratory, but during a moment of personal crisis. When faced with uncertainty and pressure, I discovered that my mind naturally organized itself into what I now recognize as an engineering control system.

"I didn't panic like most people. I built a framework. I handled the situation like a faulty system that needed a fix."

Core Insights

Fundamental principles that guide my approach to engineering

01

Structured Thinking Under Pressure

When systems fail or crises emerge, the ability to maintain structured thinking becomes crucial. Like a control tower scanning for signals, the mind must organize information systematically, eliminate variables, and track data points without letting emotions cloud judgment.

Real-world application: During my brother's disappearance, I created a mental diagnostic system that gathered data, eliminated possibilities, and maintained operational clarity.
02

Learning from System Failures

Failure isn't just about the result, it's about what you do after. Every failure contains valuable data that can inform future designs and decisions. The key is to approach failure analytically, not emotionally, treating it as a source of information rather than disappointment.

Real-world application: When my drone enhancement project resulted in overheating and fire, I analyzed every decision and component to understand why it failed.
03

Systems Thinking in Daily Life

Engineering principles extend far beyond technical projects. The same systematic approach used to design spacecraft can be applied to personal challenges, decision-making, and problem-solving in any field. Every problem is a system that can be analyzed and optimized.

Real-world application: Approaching family emergencies, academic challenges, and personal goals with the same structural framework used in engineering design processes.
04

The Beauty of Aerospace Engineering

Aerospace represents the ultimate combination of function, motion, logic, and timing. In space, there are no second chances, every system must work flawlessly, every component must serve its purpose, and every decision must be calculated and precise. This demands the highest level of engineering excellence.

Personal connection: My fascination with how signals travel, how systems fail and reboot, and how complex systems maintain functionality under extreme conditions drives my passion for aerospace engineering.

Problem-Solving Methodology

A systematic approach to engineering challenges

1

Scan for Signals

Gather all available data and information about the system or problem. Like a radar system, cast a wide net to capture all relevant inputs.

2

Eliminate Variables

Systematically rule out possibilities and narrow down the potential causes or solutions. Remove assumptions and focus on facts that can be verified.

3

Check Failure Points

Identify where systems might break down or where problems typically occur. Understand the weak links in the chain.

4

Build Solutions

Construct solutions based on the analysis. Design systems that can handle expected stresses and unexpected challenges.

5

Learn and Iterate

Extract lessons from both successes and failures. Use this knowledge to improve future designs and decision-making processes.

Looking Forward

My goal is to apply this systematic thinking approach in the field of aerospace. I want to contribute to the design of systems that must function flawlessly in the harsh weather conditions, from spacecraft navigating the vacuum of space to control systems managing complex orbital maneuvers.

The same mindset that helped me navigate a family crisis can be scaled up to help humanity navigate the challenges of space exploration. Every mission is a system, every spacecraft is a collection of interconnected components.
